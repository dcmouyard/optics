Vocabulary

absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing dark lines or bands due to absorption of specific wavelengths.

angle of deviation

The angle between the direction of the refracted ray and the direction of the incident ray when a ray of light passes from one medium to another.

angle of incidence

The angle between a ray incident on a surface and the normal.

angle of reflection

The angle between a reflected ray and the normal.

angle of refraction

The angle between a refracted ray and the normal line.

aperture

A space through which light passes in an optical or photographic instrument, especially the variable opening by which light enters a camera.

band absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing dark bands due to absorption of a specific range of wavelengths.

center of curvature

choroid

The pigmented vascular layer of the eye between the retina and sclera.

Also known as the choroidea or choroid coat.

chromatic aberration

The material effect produced by the refraction of different wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation through slightly different angles, resulting in a failure to focus. It causes colored fringes in the images produced by uncorrected lenses.

ciliary body

The part of the eye that connects the iris to the choroid. It consists of the ciliary muscle (which alters the curvature of the lens), a series of radial ciliary processes (from which the lens is suspended by ligaments), and the ciliary ring (which adjoins the choroid).

ciliary muscles

The part of the eye that alters the curvature of the lens, focusing the light.

coincident

Occuring together in space or time.

compound microscope

A type of optical microscope which uses a lens close to the object being viewed to collect light (called the objective lens) which focuses a real image of the object inside the microscope. That image is then magnified by a second lens or group of lenses (called the eyepiece) that gives the viewer an enlarged inverted virtual image of the object.

concave

Curved like the inner surface of a sphere.

concave lens

A lens where both surfaces are concave.

All concave lenses are diverging lenses.

concave mirror

A mirror with a concave surface.

All concave mirrors are converging mirrors.

cones

One of three types of photoreceptor cells in the retina.

They detect frequency of light, so are responsible for color vision.

They work best in bright light.

continuous absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing only one color due to the absorption of all other color wavelengths.

converging lens

A lens that causes parallel rays to converge inward.

converging mirror

A mirror that causes parallel rays to converge inward.

convex

Curved like the outer surface of a sphere.

cornea

corrective lens

A meniscus lens worn in front of the eye, mainly used to correct myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism, and presbyopia.

dialator muscles

The part of the eye that controls the iris and pupil, adjusting the amount of light that can enter.

diaphragm

diffuse reflection

lens

A transmissive optical device that focuses or disperses a light beam by means of refraction.

A simple lens consists of a single piece of transparent material, while a compound lens consists of several simple lenses (elements), usually arranged along a common axis.

line absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing dark lines due to absorption of one or more discrete wavelengths.

meniscus lens

A lens where one surface is concave and the other is convex.

Most commonly used as a corrective lens.

parabolic mirror

A converging mirror with a concave surface whose curvature is a parabola.

All incoming parallel rays are reflected to a single focal point, and can correct spherical aberration.

retina

The third and inner coat of the eye which is a light-sensitive layer of tissue. (Like film in a camera.)

Light striking the retina results in visual information sent to the brain via the optic nerve.

rods

One of three types of photoreceptor cells in the retina.

They detect intensity of light.

They work better than cones in dimly lit situations, and are responsible for night vision.

They are concentrated in the outer edges of the retina, and are used for peripheral vision.

sclera

The opaque, fibrous, protective, outer layer of the eye containing collagen and elastic fiber.

In humans, the sclera is white.
