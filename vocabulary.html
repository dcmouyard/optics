Vocabulary

Expand All
Abbe number

A measure of a transparent material’s dispersion. The higher the number, the lower the dispersion.

Also known as the V-number or constringence.

absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing dark lines or bands due to absorption of specific wavelengths.

The three types of absorption spectra are absorption emission spectrum, line absorption spectrum, and band absorption spectrum.

achromatic lens

An optical lens designed to limit the effects of chromatic and spherical aberrations inherent in simple (singlet) lenses. They work by refracting the outer wavelengths (typically red and blue) back to the focal point.

They are typically created by combining a biconvex lens of crown glass with a biconcave lens of flint glass.

Also known as an achromatic doublet.

angle

The space (usually measured in degrees) between two intersecting lines or surfaces at or close to the point where they meet.

Often defined as θ in diagrams and equations.

angle of deviation

The angle between the direction of the refracted ray and the direction of the incident ray when a ray of light passes from one medium to another.

angle of incidence

The angle between a ray incident on a surface and the normal.

angle of reflection

The angle between a reflected ray and the normal.

angle of refraction

The angle between a refracted ray and the normal line.

Ångström

A unit of distance (Å) equal to 1 x 10⁻⁸ meters.

anterior chamber of the eye

The fluid-filled space inside the eye between the iris and the cornea’s innermost surface.

It is located in the anterior segment of the eye and is filled with the aqueous humor.

anterior segment of the eye

The front third of the eye that includes the structures in front of the vitreous humour: the cornea, iris, ciliary body, and lens.

aperture

A space through which light passes in an optical or photographic instrument, especially the variable opening by which light enters a camera.

A camera lens aperture is usually specified as an f-number, which is the ratio of the focal length to the effective aperture diameter.

A lens typically has a set of marked “f-stops” that the f-number can be set to. A lower f-number denotes a greater aperture opening which allows more light to reach the film or image sensor.

The smaller the aperture, the longer the shutter speed must be to result in the same exposure.

aqueous humor

The clear fluid filling the space in the the front of the eyeball between the lens and the cornea.

It is primarily found in the anterior and posterior chambers, but not.

astigmatism

A defect in the eye or in a lens caused by a deviation from spherical curvature, which results in distorted and blurry images since all light rays aren’t refracted to a single focal point.

It is usually caused by an irregularly shaped cornea, which looks more like a football than a sphere.

The three main types of astimatism are myopic, hyperopic, and mixed.

band absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing dark bands due to absorption of a specific range of wavelengths.

Produced when unexcited substance in molecular state is placed between the source and the prism.

band emission spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation showing distinct bright bands of different wavelengths, each having sharp edges.

Calcium or barium salts in the Bunsen burner flame and nitrogen in molecular state in vacuum tubes give band emission spectra.

barrel distortion

Occurs when the field of view of a lens is much wider than the size of the film or image sensor and hence it needs to be “squeezed” to fit. As a result, straight lines are visibly curved inwards, especially towards the extreme edges of the frame.

Commonly seen on wide angle lenses.

beam diameter

The diameter along any specified line that is perpendicular to the electromagnetic beam axis and intersects it. Since electromagnetic beams typically do not have sharp edges, the diameter can be defined in many different ways.

Also known as beam width.

beam divergence

An angular measure of the increase in beam diameter or radius with distance from the optical aperture from which the electromagnetic beam emerges.

You can calculate the divergence of a beam if you know the beam diameter at two separate points far from any focus (Di, Df), and the distance (l) between these points.

biconcave lens

A lens where both surfaces are concave.

All biconcave lenses are diverging lenses.

Also known as a double concave lens or concave lens.

biconvex lens

A lens where both surfaces are convex.

All biconvex lenses are converging lenses.

Also known as a double convex lens or convex lens.

blueshift

A decrease in wavelength, with a corresponding increase in frequency, of an electromagnetic wave due to the Doppler effect, which shifts colors from the red end of the visible spectrum to the blue end.

This occurs when an object in space is moving towards us.

camera

An optical instrument for recording or capturing images. Comes from the word camera obscura, which means “dark chamber.”

Light enters through a converging lens, passes through an aperture, and is recorded on a light-sensitive medium such as film.

candela

A unit of luminous intensity (cd) which measures luminous power per unit solid angle emitted by a point light source in a particular direction.

center of curvature

A point that is at a distance from the curve equal to the radius of curvature lying on the normal line.

Often defined as C in diagrams and equations.

choroid

The pigmented vascular layer of the eye between the retina and sclera.

chromatic aberration

The effect produced by the refraction of different wavelengths of light through slightly different angles, resulting in a failure to focus. It causes colored fringes in the images produced by uncorrected lenses.

ciliary body

The part of the eye that connects the iris to the choroid. It consists of the ciliary muscle (which alters the curvature of the lens), a series of radial ciliary processes (from which the lens is suspended by ligaments), and the ciliary ring (which adjoins the choroid).

CMYK

A printing process that uses the CMY color model, where the letters correspond to the four inks used: cyan, magenta, yellow and key (black).

CMY color model

A subtractive color model that explains how the mixing of dyes, inks, and paint pigments create a wider range of colors, each the result of partially or completely subtracting (that is, absorbing) some wavelengths of light and not others.

Cyan, magenta, and yellow are the primary colors used to generate other colors in this model.

Mixing all colors in a subtractive model results in black.

coincident

Occuring together in space or time.

color filter

In an additive color system (such as light) a colored filter will absorb its complement and transmit the other colors.

For example, a blue colored filter will absorb yellow light, letting all other colors through. Therefor:

  • Any color light shined through a blue filter will look bluer. The amount of yellow in the original color will determine how much bluer the result is.
  • White light shined through a blue filter will look blue.
  • Yellow light shined through a blue filter will look black.
  • Blue light shined through a blue filter will look blue.
  • Orange light shined through a blue filter will look red. (Yellow + Red = Orange)
color wheel

An abstract illustrative organization of color hues around a circle, which shows the relationships between colors.

The three primary colors are equidistant from each other.

The three secondary colors are equidistant from each other, and are spaced half-way between each primary color.

There are six tertiary colors, which are half-way between each adjacent primary and secondary color.

Complementary colors are on opposite sides of the color wheel from each other.

Also known as the color circle.

complementary colors

Pairs of colors which, when combined, cancel each other out. When combined together in equal amounts they produce white in light (additive) or black in pigment (subtractive.)

When placed next to each other, they create the strongest contrast for those particular two colors.

  • Red : Cyan
  • Green : Magenta
  • Blue : Yellow

Also known as opposite colors.

compound microscope

A type of optical microscope which uses a lens close to the object being viewed to collect light (called the objective lens) which focuses a real image of the object inside the microscope. That image is then magnified by a second lens or group of lenses (called the eyepiece) that gives the viewer an enlarged inverted virtual image of the object.

concave

Curved like the inner surface of a sphere.

concave mirror

A mirror with a concave surface.

All concave mirrors are converging mirrors.

cones

One of the two main types of photoreceptor cells in the retina, and are densely packed in the fovea centralis.

They detect frequency of light, so are responsible for color vision.

They work best in bright light since they are less sensitive to the intensity of light.

They can also perceive finer detail and more rapid changes in images than rods.

There are approximately 7 million cone cells in the eye.

There are three types of cone cells (S, M, and L) which are sensitive to different ranges of light wavelengths and frequencies. S detects short wavelengths, M detects medium wavelengths, and L detects long wavelengths.

continuous absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing an absence of a continuous wavelength range.

Placing a blue color filter between white light and a prism results in a continous absorption spectrum due to the absorption of all colors except blue.

continuous emission spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation showing a wide range of unrepeated wavelengths/colors.

The spectra obtained from a luminous body like tungsten filament, burning coal, red hot metals, burning candles, stars, and the sun are continuous emission spectra.

converging lens

A lens that causes parallel rays to refract and converge inward.

converging mirror

A mirror that causes parallel rays to reflect and converge inward.

All converging mirrors are concave.

convex

Curved like the outer surface of a sphere.

convex mirror

A mirror with a convex surface.

All convex mirrors are diverging mirrors.

cornea

The transparent layer forming the front of the eye.

Light is most refracted when it enters the cornea since the index of refraction for most parts of the eye is very similar.

corrective lens

A meniscus lens worn in front of the eye, mainly used to correct myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism, and presbyopia.

critical angle

The largest possible angle of incidence which still results in a refracted ray. This refracted ray will travel along the boundary and the angle of refraction is 90°.

crown glass

Optical glass that has a relatively low index of refraction (around 1.52) and a high Abbe number (around 60) which decreases refraction and dispersion.

As well as the specific material named crown glass, there are other optical glasses with similar properties that are also called crown glasses, such as BK7. These crown glasses generally have an Abbe number between 50 and 85.

A biconvex lens of crown glass is commonly combined with a biconcave lens of flint glass to create an achromatic lens, which reduces chromatic aberration.

dialator muscles

The part of the eye that controls the iris and pupil, adjusting the amount of light that can enter.

diffuse reflection

Occurs when parallel light rays are scattered by a rough surface.

diopter

A unit of optical power (D or dpt) equal to the reciprocal of the focal length (in meters) of a given lens. (equivalent to 1/m or m⁻¹)

dispersion

The phenomenon in which the velocity of a wave depends on its frequency. This occurs for light in any medium that has an index of refraction greater than one (i.e., not in a vacuum.)

Since colors have different frequencies, they travel at different speeds, and thus they refract at different angles.

dispersive prism

An optical prism (usually in the shape of a triangle) that disperses light into separate colors.

Because colors travel at different speeds, they refract at different angles.

diverging lens

A lens that causes parallel rays to refract and diverge outward.

Forms virtual images.

diverging mirror

A lens that causes parallel rays to reflect and diverge outward.

All diverging mirrors are convex.

Forms virtual images.

Doppler effect

The change in frequency of a wave as the source and observer move toward or away from each other.

When the source and observor are moving towards each other, the frequency increases. When they are moving away from each other the frequency decreases.

When looking at object in space, this effect results in redshift or blueshift.

dye

A colored substance that has an affinity to the substrate to which it is being applied. Like dyes, they are colored because they absorb some wavelengths of light more than others.

emission spectrum

Spectrum of frequencies of electromagnetic radiation emitted by an element’s atoms or a compound’s molecules when they are returned to a lower energy state.

The three types of emission spectra are continuous emission spectrum, line emission spectrum, and band emission spectrum.

energy

A property of objects which can be transferred to other objects or converted into different forms, usually measured in Joules.

Often defined as E in diagrams and equations.

exposure

The amount of light per unit area (the image plane illuminance times the exposure time) reaching a photographic film or electronic image sensor, as determined by shutter speed, lens aperture, and scene luminance.

Exposure is measured in lux seconds, and can be computed from exposure value (EV) and scene luminance in a specified region.

In a camera, the exposure determines how bright or dark an image looks.

eyepiece

A lens or group of lenses in an optical system that is closest to the eye.

In a compound microscope it gives the viewer an enlarged inverted virtual image of the object, which was focused by the objective lens.

far point

The farthest point from the eye at which images are clear.

farsightedness

A type of refractive error where distant objects may be seen more clearly than objects that are near.

Occurs when the focal point of the eye is behind the retina, due to he eye being overly flat or too short.

A converging lens is used to fixed farsightedness.

Also known as hyperopia.

film

A strip or sheet of transparent plastic film base coated on one side with a gelatin emulsion containing microscopically small light-sensitive silver halide crystals.

Used to record an image in a camera.

flint glass

Optical glass that has a relatively high index of refraction (between 1.45 and 2.00) and a low Abbe number (55 or less) which increases refractionand dispersion.

Commonly used for dispersive prisms.

A biconcave lens of flint glass is commonly combined with a biconvex lens of crown glass to create an achromatic lens, which reduces chromatic aberration.

focal length

The distance between the focal point and the vertex of a spherical lens or mirror.

The focal length of a spherical lens or mirror is always equal to half of its radius of curvature.

Often defined as f in diagrams and equations.

focal plane

The plane through the focal point perpendicular to the axis of a mirror or lens.

focal point

The point where light rays parallel and close to the principal axis converge to or appear to diverge from, which is halfway between the vertex and the center of curvature of a spherical mirror.

Often defined as f or F in diagrams and equations.

Also known as the principal focus.

fovea centralis

A small depression in the retina of the eye where visual acuity is highest. The center of the field of vision is focused in this region, where retinal cones are particularly concentrated.

frequency

The number of occurrences of a repeating event per unit time, usually measured in Hertz.

For waves, it’s the number of wave cycles passing a fixed reference point in one second.

Often defined as f or ν in diagrams and equations.

Galilean telescope

A refracting telescope with a weak converging objective lens and a strong diverging eyepiece lens. The lenses are separated by the difference of their focal lengths.

Results in an upright virtual image.

gamma rays

Electromagnetic radiation with the shortest wavelengths, highest frequencies, and most energy in the electromagnetic spectrum.

They are produced by the hottest and most energetic objects in the universe, such as neutron stars, pulsars, supernova explosions, and regions around black holes.

Wavelengths are below 10pm.

Frequencies are above 30EHz.

Also known as gamma radiation.

glancing angle

The angle between a ray incident on a surface and the surface, which equals 90° minus the angle of incidence.

Also known as a grazing angle.

Gregorian telescope

A reflecting telescope with a secondary concave mirror at the first mirror’s focal point, which directs light through a hole in the first mirror’s center.

Results in an upright real image.

Hertz

A unit of frequency (Hz) equal to one cycle per second. (equivalent to 1/s or s⁻¹)

illuminance

The total luminous flux incident on a surface per unit area, usually measured in lux.

index of refraction

A dimensionless number that describes how light propagates through a medium.

The index of refraction for a medium is equal to the speed of light in a vacuum divided by the speed of light in that medium.

The refractive index of a medium is constant for every angle at which light can enter, but it is different for every wavelength of light.

Often defined as n in diagrams and equations.

infrared

Electromagnetic radiation with shorter wavelengths than microwaves and longer wavelengths than visible light in the electromagnetic spectrum. Often abbreviated as IR.

Wavelengths are between 700nm and 1mm.

Frequencies are between 300GHz and 430THz.

iris

A thin, circular structure in the eye responsible for controlling the diameter and size of the pupil, and thus the amount of light reaching the retina.

Contains pigment which determines a person’s eye color, such as blue or brown.

Joule

A unit of energy (J) equal to the energy transferred to (or work done on) an object when a force of one newton acts on that object in the direction of its motion through a distance of one meter. (equivalent to N·m)

Keplerian telescope

A refracting telescope with a weak converging objective lens and a strong converging eyepiece lens. The lenses are separated by the sum of their focal lengths.

Has a larger field of view than a Galilean telescope.

Results in an inverted real image.

law of reflection

The angle of reflection is equal to the angle of incidence.

lens

A transmissive optical device that focuses or disperses a light beam by means of refraction.

A simple lens consists of a single piece of transparent material, while a compound lens consists of several simple lenses (elements), usually arranged along a common axis.

The lens of an eye sits behind the pupil between the anterior and posterior chambers. It changes shape to adjust its focal distance so that the eye can focus on objects at different distances.

line absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing dark lines due to absorption of one or more discrete wavelengths.

line emission spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation showing one or more distinct bright lines of different wavelengths.

The line emission spectra is produced by an excited substance in atomic state, such as sodium vapour lamp, gases in discharge tube, mercury vapour lamp, luminous helium, argon, and neon.

lumen

A unit of luminous flux (lm) equal to the luminous flux of light produced by a light source that emits one candela of luminous intensity over a solid angle of one steradian.

luminance

The luminous intensity per unit area of light travelling in a given direction, usually measured in candelas per meter squared (cd/m².)

An indicator of how bright the surface will appear. It describes the amount of light that passes through, is emitted, or reflected from a particular area and falls within a given solid angle.

luminous flux

The perceived power of light, usually measured in lumens.

Often defined as Φ in diagrams and equations.

Also known as luminous power.

luminous intensity

The wavelength-weighted power emitted by a light source in a particular direction per unit solid angle, usually measured in candelas.

Based on the luminosity function, a standardized model of the sensitivity of the human eye.

magnification

meniscus lens

A lens where one surface is concave and the other is convex.

Most commonly used as a corrective lens.

This lens can be either converging or diverging, depending on the relative curvature of each side.

A negative meniscus lens has a steeper concave surface and is thinner at the center than at the periphery. Conversely, a positive meniscus lens has a steeper convex surface and is thicker at the centre than at the periphery.

Also known as a concavo-convex lens or convexo-concave lens.

meter

A unit of distance (m) equal to the distance travelled by light in a vacuum in 1/299792458 seconds.

metric prefix

A metric prefix is a unit prefix that precedes a basic unit of measure to indicate a multiple or fraction of the unit.

microwave

Electromagnetic radiation with shorter wavelengths than radio and longer wavelengths than infrared in the electromagnetic spectrum.

Wavelengths are between 1mm and 1m.

Frequencies are between 300MHz and 300GHz.

monochromatic

Electromagnetic radiation of a single wavelength or frequency. For visible light this means all of the waves are of the same wavelength.

muscae volitantes

Microscopic particles of cellular debris in the eye that freely float in the vitreous humor.

Also known as floaters.

near point

The nearest point to the eye at which images are clear.

nearsightedness

A type of refractive error where near objects may be seen more clearly than objects that are distant.

Occurs when the focal point of the eye is in front of the retina, due to the eye being too curved or too long.

A diverging lens is used to fixed nearsightedness.

Also known as myopia.

Newtonian telescope

A reflecting telescope with an angled flat secondary mirror that directs light to the side of the first mirror.

Results in a real image.

normal

The line perpendicular to the surface at the point of incidence.

Also known as the normal line.

objective lens

A lens close to the object being viewed to collect light in a compound microscope which focuses a real image of the object inside the microscope.

opaque

A medium’s property that prevents electromagnetic radiation from travelling through it. If something is opaque to the visible light spectrum, then you can’t see through it.

optic nerve

A paired nerve that sends visual information from the retina to the brain.

optical density

A measure of how well a medium can transmit light.

The higher a medium’s optical density, the slower light travels in it, and the higher its index of refraction.

optical instrument

An object that either processes light waves to enhance an image for viewing, or analyzes light waves (or photons) to determine one of a number of characteristic properties.

optical microscope

A type of microscope which uses visible light and a system of lenses to magnify images of small samples.

optical power

The degree to which a lens, mirror, or other optical system converges or diverges light, usually measured in diopters. Thus this is another way of describing how much this lens, mirror, or optical system magnifies images.

Converging lenses have positive optical power, while diverging lenses have negative optical power.

For two or more thin lenses close together, the optical power of the combined lenses is approximately equal to the sum of the optical powers of each lens: P = P₁ + P₂. Similarly, the optical power of a single lens is roughly equal to the sum of the powers of each surface. These approximations are commonly used in optometry.

Often defined as P in diagrams and equations.

parabolic mirror

A converging mirror with a concave surface whose curvature is a parabola.

All incoming parallel rays are reflected to a single focal point, and can correct spherical aberration in telescopes.

phase velocity

The rate at which the phase of a wave propagates in space. This is the velocity at which the phase of any single, discrete frequency component of a wave travels.

photoreceptor cell

A specialized type of neuron found in the retina that is capable of phototransduction, meaning it can convert light (visible electromagnetic radiation) into signals that can stimulate biological processes.

The two classic types of photoreceptor cells are rods and cones.

pigment

A colored substance that is insoluble and has no affinity to the substrate to which it is being applied. Like dyes, they are colored because they absorb some wavelengths of light more than others.

pinhole camera

A camera with a pinhole aperture and no lens.

plane mirror

A mirror with a flat (planar) reflective surface.

All incoming parallel light rays will still be parallel after reflection.

The image formed by a plane mirror is always virtual, upright, and of the same shape and size as the object it is reflecting.

plano-concave lens

A lens where one surface is a plane and the other is concave.

All plano-concave lenses are diverging lenses.

plano-convex lens

A lens where one surface is a plane and the other is convex.

All plano-convex lenses are converging lenses.

polarized light

polarizing prism

Polaroid filter

posterior chamber of the eye

The narrow space behind the peripheral part of the iris, and in front of the suspensory ligament of the lens and the ciliary processes.

It is located in the anterior segment of the eye and is filled with the aqueous humor.

posterior segment of the eye

The back two-thirds of the eye that includes the anterior hyaloid membrane and all of the optical structures behind it: the vitreous humor, retina, choroid, and optic nerve.

primary colors for light (additive)

Red, green, and blue. Mixing equal amounts of all three together will produce white.

primary colors for pigment (subtractive)

Cyan, magents, and yellow. Mixing equal amounts of all three together will produce black.

principal axis

A line passing through the center of curvature to the exact center of a lens or spherical mirror.

This line is parallel to the axis of symmetry.

Also known as an optical axis.

prism

A transparent optical element with flat, polished surfaces that refract light. At least two of the flat surfaces must have an angle between them.

Different types of prisms can disperse light into separate colors, reflect light, or split light into components with different polarizations.

Typical materials include glass, plastic, and fluorite.

pupil

The hole located in the center of the iris of the eye that allows light to strike the retina.

radio waves

Electromagnetic radiation with the longest wavelengths, lowest frequencies, and least energy in the electromagnetic spectrum.

Wavelengths are above 1m.

Frequencies are below 300MHz.

radius of curvature

The distance between the center of curvature and the vertex of a spherical lens or mirror.

The radius of the circular arc which best approximates the curve at a point on a spherical surface.

Often defined as R in diagrams and equations.

ray diagram

A tracing of light rays to show where the image forms after being reflected off a mirror or refracted through a lens.

real image

An image which is located in the plane of convergence for the light rays that originate from a given object. If a screen is placed in the plane of a real image the image will generally become visible on the screen.

Also known as a real object.

redshift

An increase in wavelength, with a corresponding decrease in frequency, of an electromagnetic wave due to the Doppler effect, which shifts colors from the blue end of the visible spectrum to the red end.

This occurs when an object in space is moving away from us.

reflecting telescope

An optical telescope that uses one or more curved mirrors as its objective to form an image.

Also known as a catoptric telescope.

reflective prism

refracting telescope

An optical telescope that uses a lens as its objective to form an image.

The two main types of refracting telescopes are Galilean and Keplerian.

Suffers from severe chromatic aberration.

refraction

The change in direction of light due to a change in its transmission medium.

retina

The third and inner coat of the eye which is a light-sensitive layer of tissue. (Like film in a camera.)

Light striking the retina results in visual information sent to the brain via the optic nerve.

RGB color model

An additive color model that explains how the mixing of different colored lights combine to create a wider range of colors, each the result of partially or completely adding some wavelengths of light and not others.

Red, green, and blue are the primary colors used to generate other colors in this model.

Mixing all colors in an additive model results in white.

rods

One of the two main types of photoreceptor cells in the retina.

They detect intensity of light.

They work better than cones in dimly lit situations, and are responsible for night vision.

There are approximately 120 million rod cells in the eye.

They are concentrated in the outer edges of the retina and are used for peripheral vision.

sclera

The opaque, fibrous, protective, outer layer of the eye containing collagen and elastic fiber.

In humans, the sclera is white.

secondary axis

Any line drawn through the center of curvature to the mirror or lens.

shutter

A device that allows light to pass for a determined period, exposing photographic film or a light-sensitive sensor to light in order to capture a permanent image of a scene.

shutter speed

The time for which a shutter remains open, which controls exposure time in a camera.

The longer the shutter speed, the shorter the aperture must be to result in the same exposure.

simple lens

A lens consisting of a single simple element. Typical examples include a magnifying glass or a lens in a pair of simple reading glasses.

They are prone to aberrations, especially chromatic aberration.

Also known as a singlet lens.

Snell’s law

Formula used to describe the relationship between the angle of incidence, the angle of refraction, and the indices of refraction.

For monochromatic light, it also describes the relationship between the wavelength and velocity of the light before and after refraction.

Also known as the law of refraction.

spectrum

specular reflection

The mirror-like reflection of light from a smooth surface.

speed of light in a vacuum
  • 2.99792458 x 10⁸ m/s
  • 186,282 mi/s
spherical aberration

The loss of definition in an image due to the surface geometry of a lens or mirror, which causes incoming parallel rays to focus at different points.

total internal reflection

If an incident ray is larger than the critical angle and the refractive index of the current medium is higher than the refractive index of the adjoining medium, then all of the rays will reflect and remain in the current medium.

translucent

A medium’s property that allows some electromagnetic radiation to travel through it. If something is translucent to the visible light spectrum, then you can partially see through it, but not clearly.

transparent

A medium’s property that allows electromagnetic radiation to travel through it. If something is transparent to the visible light spectrum, then you can clearly see through it.

ultraviolet

Electromagnetic radiation with shorter wavelengths than visible light and longer wavelengths than x-rays in the electromagnetic spectrum. Often abbreviated as UV.

Wavelengths are between 10nm and 400nm.

Frequencies are between 750THz and 30PHz.

vertex

A point where two or more curves, lines, or edges meet.

The point on a spherical surface where the principal axis meets the surface, which is the geometric center of a mirror or lens surface.

virtual image

An image formed when the outgoing rays from a point on an object always diverge. The image appears to be located at the point of apparent divergence. Because the rays never really converge, a virtual image cannot be projected onto a screen.

In diagrams of optical systems, virtual rays are conventionally represented by dotted lines. Virtual images are located by tracing the real rays that emerge from an optical device (lens, mirror, or some combination) backward to a perceived point of origin.

visible light spectrum

The portion of the electromagnetic spectrum that is visible to the human eye.

Wavelengths are between 400nm (violet) to 700nm (red).

Frequencies are between 428THz (red) and 749THz (violet).

vitreous humor

The clear gel that fills the space between the lens and the retina of the eye. It helps to keep the retina in place by pressing it against the choroid.

Also known as the vitreous body.

wavelength

The distance between two successive crests of a wave.

The wavelength is equal to the speed of the wave divided by the frequency the wave.

Often defined as λ in diagrams and equations.

x-rays

Electromagnetic radiation with shorter wavelengths than ultraviolet and longer wavelengths than gamma rays in the electromagnetic spectrum.

Wavelengths are between 10pm and 10nm.

Frequencies are between 30PHz and 30EHz.
