Vocabulary Expand All

Abbe number

A measure of a transparent material’s dispersion. The higher the number, the lower the dispersion.

Also known as the V-number or constringence.

absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing dark lines or bands due to absorption of specific wavelengths.

achromatic doublet

angle

The space (usually measured in degrees) between two intersecting lines or surfaces at or close to the point where they meet.

Often defined as θ in diagrams and equations.

angle of deviation

The angle between the direction of the refracted ray and the direction of the incident ray when a ray of light passes from one medium to another.

angle of incidence

The angle between a ray incident on a surface and the normal.

angle of reflection

The angle between a reflected ray and the normal.

angle of refraction

The angle between a refracted ray and the normal line.

Ångström

A unit of distance (Å) equal to 1 x 10-8 meters.

anterior chamber

aperture

A space through which light passes in an optical or photographic instrument, especially the variable opening by which light enters a camera.

A camera lens aperture is usually specified as an f-number, which is the ratio of the focal length to the effective aperture diameter.

A lens typically has a set of marked “f-stops” that the f-number can be set to. A lower f-number denotes a greater aperture opening which allows more light to reach the film or image sensor.

The smaller the aperture, the longer the shutter speed must be to result in the same exposure.

aqueous humor

astigmatism

band absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing dark bands due to absorption of a specific range of wavelengths.

barrel distortion

beam diameter

biconcave lens

A lens where both surfaces are concave.

All biconcave lenses are diverging lenses.

biconvex lens

A lens where both surfaces are convex.

All biconvex lenses are converging lenses.

blue shift

camera

An optical instrument for recording or capturing images. Comes from the word camera obscura, which means “dark chamber.”

Light enters through a converging lens, passes through an aperture, and is recorded on a light-sensitive medium such as film.

candela

A unit of luminous intensity (cd) which measures luminous power per unit solid angle emitted by a point light source in a particular direction.

Cartesion surface

center of curvature

A point that is at a distance from the curve equal to the radius of curvature lying on the normal line.

Often defined as C in diagrams and equations.

choroid

The pigmented vascular layer of the eye between the retina and sclera.

chromatic aberration

The effect produced by the refraction of different wavelengths of light through slightly different angles, resulting in a failure to focus. It causes colored fringes in the images produced by uncorrected lenses.

ciliary body

The part of the eye that connects the iris to the choroid. It consists of the ciliary muscle (which alters the curvature of the lens), a series of radial ciliary processes (from which the lens is suspended by ligaments), and the ciliary ring (which adjoins the choroid).

CMYK color model

coincident

Occuring together in space or time.

complementary colors

compound microscope

A type of optical microscope which uses a lens close to the object being viewed to collect light (called the objective lens) which focuses a real image of the object inside the microscope. That image is then magnified by a second lens or group of lenses (called the eyepiece) that gives the viewer an enlarged inverted virtual image of the object.

concave

Curved like the inner surface of a sphere.

concave mirror

A mirror with a concave surface.

All concave mirrors are converging mirrors.

cones

One of the two main types of photoreceptor cells in the retina, and are densely packed in the fovea centralis.

They detect frequency of light, so are responsible for color vision.

They work best in bright light since they are less sensitive to the intensity of light.

They can also perceive finer detail and more rapid changes in images than rods.

There are three types of cone cells, which are sensitive to different ranges of light wavelengths and frequencies.

continuous absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing only one color due to the absorption of all other color wavelengths.

converging lens

A lens that causes parallel rays to refract and converge inward.

converging mirror

A mirror that causes parallel rays to reflect and converge inward.

All converging mirrors are concave.

convex

Curved like the outer surface of a sphere.

convex mirror

A mirror with a convex surface.

All convex mirrors are diverging mirrors.

cornea

The transparent layer forming the front of the eye.

Light is most refracted when it enters the cornea since the index of refraction for most parts of the eye is very similar.

corrective lens

A meniscus lens worn in front of the eye, mainly used to correct myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism, and presbyopia.

critical angle

The largest possible angle of incidence which still results in a refracted ray. This refracted ray will travel along the boundary and the angle of refraction is 90°.

crown glass

Optical glass that has a relatively low index of refraction (around 1.52) and a high Abbe number (around 60) which decreases refraction and dispersion.

As well as the specific material named crown glass, there are other optical glasses with similar properties that are also called crown glasses, such as BK7. These crown glasses generally have an Abbe number between 50 and 85.

A biconvex lens of crown glass is commonly combined with a biconcave lens of flint glass to create an achromatic doublet, which reduces chromatic aberration.

dialator muscles

The part of the eye that controls the iris and pupil, adjusting the amount of light that can enter.

diffuse reflection

Occurs when parallel light rays are scattered by a rough surface.

diopter

discrete

dispersion

The phenomenon in which the velocity of a wave depends on its frequency. This occurs for light in any medium that has an index of refraction greater than one (i.e., not in a vacuum.)

Since colors have different frequencies, they travel at different speeds, and thus they refract at different angles.

dispersive prism

An optical prism (usually in the shape of a triangle) that disperses light into separate colors.

Because colors travel at different speeds, they refract at different angles.

diverging lens

A lens that causes parallel rays to refract and diverge outward.

Forms virtual images.

diverging mirror

A lens that causes parallel rays to reflect and diverge outward.

All diverging mirrors are convex.

Forms virtual images.

doppler effect

dye

emission spectrum

Spectrum of frequencies of electromagnetic radiation emitted by an element’s atoms or a compound’s molecules when they are returned to a lower energy state.

Also known as a bright line spectrum.

energy

exposure

The amount of light per unit area (the image plane illuminance times the exposure time) reaching a photographic film or electronic image sensor, as determined by shutter speed, lens aperture, and scene luminance.

Exposure is measured in lux seconds, and can be computed from exposure value (EV) and scene luminance in a specified region.

In a camera, the exposure determines how bright or dark an image looks.

eyepiece

A second lens or group of lenses in a compound microscope that gives the viewer an enlarged inverted virtual image of the object, which was focused by the objective lens.

far point

far-sightedness

film

flint glass

Optical glass that has a relatively high index of refraction (between 1.45 and 2.00) and a low Abbe number (55 or less) which increases refractionand dispersion.

Commonly used for dispersive prisms.

A biconcave lens of flint glass is commonly combined with a biconvex lens of crown glass to create an achromatic doublet, which reduces chromatic aberration.

focal length

The distance between the focal point and the vertex of a spherical lens or mirror.

The focal length of a spherical lens or mirror is always equal to half of its radius of curvature.

focal plane

focal point

The point halfway between the vertex and the center of curvature of a spherical mirror.

All light travelling parallel to the principal axis of a spherical lens or mirror will meet at the focal point after reflection or refraction.

Often defined as f in diagrams and equations.

fovea centralis

frequency

The number of occurrences of a repeating event per unit time, usually measured in Hertz.

For waves, it’s the number of wave cycles passing a fixed reference point in one second.

Often defined as ν in diagrams and equations.

fundamental frequency

gamma rays

Hertz

A unit of frequency (Hz) equal to one cycle per second. (equivalent to 1/s or s-1)

  • 1 THz = 1 x 1012 Hz
  • 1 GHz = 1 x 109 Hz
  • 1 MHz = 1 x 106 Hz
  • 1 kHz = 1 x 103 Hz
illuminance

The total luminous flux incident on a surface per unit area, usually measured in lux.

index of refraction

The index of refraction for a medium is equal to the speed of light in a vacuum divided by the speed of light in that medium.

lens

A transmissive optical device that focuses or disperses a light beam by means of refraction.

A simple lens consists of a single piece of transparent material, while a compound lens consists of several simple lenses (elements), usually arranged along a common axis.

The lens of an eye sits behind the pupil between the anterior and posterior chambers and focuses light on the nerve cells of the retina.

line absorption spectrum

A spectrum of electromagnetic radiation transmitted through a substance, showing dark lines due to absorption of one or more discrete wavelengths.

luminance

A photometric measure of the luminous intensity per unit area of light travelling in a given direction. It describes the amount of light that passes through, is emitted, or reflected from a particular area and falls within a given solid angle.

An indicator of how bright the surface will appear.

Usually measured in candelas per meter squared (cd/m2.)

magnification

meniscus lens

A lens where one surface is concave and the other is convex.

Most commonly used as a corrective lens.

meter

A unit of distance (m) equal to the distance travelled by light in a vacuum in 1/299792458 seconds.

  • 1 km = 1 x 103 m
  • 1 cm = 1 x 10-2 m
  • 1 mm = 1 x 10-3 m
  • 1 μm = 1 x 10-6 m
  • 1 nm = 1 x 10-9 m
  • 1 pm = 1 x 10-12 m

monochromatic

muscae volitantes

Also known as floaters.

near-sightedness

normal

Also known as the normal line.

parabolic mirror

A converging mirror with a concave surface whose curvature is a parabola.

All incoming parallel rays are reflected to a single focal point, and can correct spherical aberration.

principal axis

A line passing through the center of curvature to the exact center of a lens or spherical mirror.

This line is parallel to the axis of symmetry.

Also known as an optical axis.

prism

A transparent optical element with flat, polished surfaces that refract light. At least two of the flat surfaces must have an angle between them.

Different types of prisms can disperse light into separate colors, reflect light, or split light into components with different polarizations.

Typical materials include glass, plastic, and fluorite.

The radius of the circular arc which best approximates the curve at a point on a spherical surface.

retina

The third and inner coat of the eye which is a light-sensitive layer of tissue. (Like film in a camera.)

Light striking the retina results in visual information sent to the brain via the optic nerve.

reversibility

rods

One of the two main types of photoreceptor cells in the retina.

They detect intensity of light.

They work better than cones in dimly lit situations, and are responsible for night vision.

They are concentrated in the outer edges of the retina, and are used for peripheral vision.

sclera

The opaque, fibrous, protective, outer layer of the eye containing collagen and elastic fiber.

In humans, the sclera is white.

shutter

A device that allows light to pass for a determined period, exposing photographic film or a light-sensitive sensor to light in order to capture a permanent image of a scene.

shutter speed

The time for which a shutter remains open, which controls exposure time in a camera.

The longer the shutter speed, the shorter the aperture must be to result in the same exposure.

simple lens

A lens consisting of a single simple element. Typical examples include a magnifying glass or a lens in a pair of simple reading glasses.

They are prone to aberrations, especially chromatic aberration.

Also known as a singlet lens.

skew

Snell’s law

Formula used to describe the relationship between the angle of incidence, the angle of refraction, and the indices of refraction.

For monochromatic light, it also describes the relationship between the wavelengths and velocities of the light before and after refraction.

Also known as the law of refraction.

spherical aberration

The loss of definition in an image due to the surface geometry of a lens or mirror, which causes incoming parallel rays to focus at different points.

superposition principle

total internal reflection

If an incident ray is larger than the critical angle and the refractive index of the current medium is higher than the refractive index of the adjoining medium, then all of the rays will reflect and remain in the current medium.

The point on a spherical surface where the principal axis meets the surface, which is the geometric center of a mirror.

visible light spectrum

The portion of the electromagnetic spectrum that is visible to the human eye.

The range of wavelengths is approximately 400nm (violet) to 700nm (red).

wavelength

The distance between two successive crests of a wave.

The wavelength is equal to the speed of the wave divided by the frequency the wave.

Often defined as λ in diagrams and equations.
